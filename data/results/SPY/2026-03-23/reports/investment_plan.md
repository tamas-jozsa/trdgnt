I’m siding with the bear here, and my call is Sell.

The bull made a reasonable case for a tradable bounce: SPY is oversold with RSI around 30, the 50-day is still above the 200-day, and price is only about one ATR below the 200-day, so a mean-reversion rally is absolutely possible. I also agree with the bull that if this heavy-volume selloff turns out to be capitulation, the rebound could be sharp.

But the bear has the stronger argument for what to do right now. The market is not just “a little weak” — it’s below the 200-day, below the 10EMA, below the VWMA, and well below the 50-day. That is a stacked set of broken levels, and all of them now sit overhead as resistance. Momentum is negative with MACD below zero and the histogram still weak, volume on the selloff was very large, and ATR has expanded, which means volatility and stop risk are both rising. In that setup, buying because something is oversold is usually how people get trapped in a correction that lasts longer than expected.

So my decision is Sell, which in practice means reduce long exposure now, do not add fresh unhedged longs, and only re-enter on proof of technical repair.

Why that conclusion makes sense is simple: the bull’s case depends on what might happen next, while the bear’s case is grounded in what has already happened. Right now, price has lost key support and has not yet reclaimed anything important. Oversold is not a catalyst by itself. Heavy down-volume is not bullish by itself. A still-positive 50-over-200 relationship helps the longer-term backdrop, but it does not override an active breakdown. In markets like this, I’d rather miss the first leg of a rebound than absorb avoidable downside trying to catch it early.

Since there aren’t any prior mistake notes provided, the main improvement I’d apply from similar situations is this: don’t confuse “possible bounce” with “good entry.” I’ve learned that when price is below every major short-term reference and volatility is expanding, waiting for confirmation is usually the better risk-adjusted move. That’s especially true in index products, where failed reclaim attempts can be excellent tells.

Here’s the investment plan I’d give the trader.

If you already own SPY, cut the position down now. I would trim meaningfully rather than lightly because the tape is still damaged. If this is a core portfolio holding and you don’t want to reduce outright, then hedge it — buy puts, put on a collar, or use a small inverse ETF position against part of the exposure. The point is to stop treating this as a routine dip until the chart improves.

If you are flat, do not buy here just because RSI is near 30. Wait. The first level that matters is a close back above the 200-day around 656.5. Even that alone is not enough for me. I’d want to see follow-through above the 10EMA/VWMA area around 664 to 668, ideally on stronger upside volume and with the MACD histogram beginning to improve. If that happens, you can revisit the long side. Until then, patience is a position.

If you are an active trader and want to express the bearish view, the cleaner trade is to sell failed rallies into that 664 to 668 zone, not to chase fresh weakness at the lows. Use tight ATR-aware risk. With ATR around 9.4, you need to respect that noise is wider now. A failed retest near resistance with a stop a bit above the rejection area is much better than shorting a panic candle in the hole.

For downside management, I’d watch 648.6 closely. If that area breaks decisively, the path toward roughly 640 opens up and the bearish case strengthens. If instead SPY quickly reclaims 656.5 and then 668 on convincing volume, I’d stop pressing the bearish view and move to neutral, possibly bullish after confirmation. But that is not the current setup.

So the actionable version is this: Sell or reduce now, hedge what you keep, avoid new unhedged longs, and only turn constructive after SPY proves it can reclaim the 200-day and then the 664 to 668 resistance band with volume support. Right now the bear case is simply better supported by the actual evidence on the chart.
